The Flaky Network (120 minutes) 

The problem 

A source produces one 160-byte frame every 20 milliseconds - think live audio. Frames must 
cross a network and be "played" on a steady 20 ms clock at the other end. The network 
between is flaky: it drops packets, delays them by random amounts, reorders them, and 
sometimes delivers them twice. 

You write the two programs at either edge - the sender and the receiver - in C, C++, Golang or 
Rust. Make playout survive the network with as little added delay as possible. This is the 
tradeoff every real-time system on the internet lives on. 

Architecture (all on 127.0.0.1, all UDP) 

None

YOUR RECEIVER --47020--> harness player 

                              ^                                        

| 

                              47004 <---------- relay <----------- 

47003        (feedback path, optional) 

●  The harness source hands your sender frame i at exactly t0 + i*20ms. You cannot send 

a frame early; you don't have it yet. 

●  The relay is the hostile network (drops, jitter, reordering, duplicates). Media and 

feedback both pass through it. 

●  The harness player judges frame i at deadline t0 + DELAY_MS + i*20ms: it must have 

arrived on port 47020, correct and on time, or it is a miss. 

●  Harness legs (47010 in, 47020 out) use a fixed format: 4-byte big-endian unsigned 

seq + 160-byte payload. Between your sender and receiver 
(47001/47002/47003/47004) the wire format is entirely your design.

Language: C, C++, Golang or Rust - your choice. The C baselines compile as C++; port to 
Rust if you prefer. Your submission must build with make into ./sender and ./receiver (a 
Makefile that shells out to cargo is fine). 

Requirements: Linux, macOS, or WSL; a C/C++ compiler or Rust toolchain; Python 3.10+ for 
the harness. Run the baseline right now: 

None

make && python3 run.py --profile profiles/A.json --delay_ms 40 

It will be INVALID - every dropped packet is a glitch. Your hour starts there. 

The score 

●  Deadline-miss rate must be ≤ 1% for a run to count. 
●  Bandwidth overhead - every byte through the relay, both directions, including packets it 

then drops - must be ≤ 2.0× the raw stream. 

●  Playout delay (--delay_ms) - among valid runs, lowest wins. 

We grade with the same harness on network profiles you have not seen (different loss 
patterns, different jitter behavior). Tune for the mechanism, not for profile A. Your processes 
receive T0, DURATION_S, DELAY_MS as environment variables and are killed when the run 
ends. 

Things worth thinking about (not a checklist) 

1.  What does a lost packet cost at 20 ms per frame, and what are the only two families of 

remedies?  

2.  What does a resend cost in time when the request AND the reply each cross the hostile 

network?  

3.  What could you send ahead of time instead, and what does the 2× budget buy?  

 
4.  How big must your jitter buffer be for the delay spikes you actually measured - and what 

happens to every strategy when losses arrive in bursts?  

5.  And since you're in C/C++/ Rust: who owns each buffer, what's in network byte order, 

and does your receive loop ever block at the wrong moment? 

Deliverables 

1.  Source for your sender and receiver + Makefile (make → ./sender, ./receiver) 
2.  RUNLOG.md - per experiment: profile, delay_ms, miss %, overhead, what you changed 

and why. Graded. 

3.  NOTES.md - max 10 sentences: your design, the delay_ms we should grade at, and 

what breaks it. 

4.  SUMMARY.html- Created by your coding agent that summarizes the architecture and 

the design choices you took, and explains the architecture in detail in terms of what was 
done and going to the system level.  

Rules 

●  Standard library of your language only (sockets, threads, time). No networking 

frameworks. 

●  No side channels: your two processes talk ONLY through the relay ports. 
●  Harness files are replaced with our copies at grading; modifying them does nothing. 
●  AI coding assistants allowed. We know their unassisted score; your grade lives above it 

and in a 5-minute discussion after. 

Suggested hour 

●  0–10: build, run baseline on A and B, read the numbers. Understand WHY it fails before 

typing. 

●  10–40: fix loss first (get VALID at a generous delay), then shrink delay. One change per 

run (~35 s each); log everything. 

●  40–55: stress on B, find your lowest valid delay, lock it. 
●  55–60: RUNLOG.md, NOTES.md, state your delay_ms.
